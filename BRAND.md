## 1. Positioning

**One-line positioning:** *Industry-specific video craft and art, delivered at scale.*

This is the differentiated middle ground — premium craft + at-scale operational depth + sector-specific knowledge — that almost no competitor occupies.

**Why this matters:**
- Pure craft boutiques (Buck, Ordinary Folk) signal "bespoke, expensive" — but have no sector pages, no subscription, no operator architecture.
- Volume operators (Motion The Agency, Superside) have the structure — but signal "affordable, commodity."
- Seven Bison has to read as **both**: serious craft from the first frame, with the architecture and proof points of an operator handling ongoing relationships at scale.

**Core positioning copy (approved):**
> Premium video, motion design and animation for B2B brands with complex, regulated, or hard-to-photograph subject matter. End-to-end creative partner — from research, strategy and concept through to final delivery. AI-native production, sector-specific craft.

## 4. The "at scale" signal — what to use, what to reject

Seven Bison cannot use the standard operator-tier signals without erosion of the premium-craft position. This trade table is **load-bearing** — get it wrong and the brand drifts toward commodity.

| Standard operator signals — REJECT | Seven Bison's signals — USE |
|---|---|
| Headcount claims ("team of 50+") | Named blue-chip brand logos at volume |
| Volume claims ("500+ videos delivered") | Sector subpage depth — vocabulary that proves insider knowledge |
| Pricing visible ("from £X/month") | Studio Access named as a first-class offering with its own page |
| Money-back guarantees / free samples | Founder credibility (Bloomberg + Getty news editorial background) |
| Glossy "team" pages with stock-style photos | Customer stories with quantified outcomes from existing relationships |

## 5. Voice — extended

The five voice principles are defined in `DESIGN.master.md` section 5. This section expands on how each shows up in actual copy.

### Confident
Speaks with authority earned through experience. Doesn't ask permission. Doesn't soften with "we'd love to" or "we're excited to".

> ✅ "We deliver enterprise-ready video, end-to-end."
> ❌ "We'd be thrilled to help you with your video needs."

### Sober
Real, grounded, free of exaggeration. No hype words. No exclamation marks. Adjectives only when they earn their place.

> ✅ "Launch-ready video in 48 hours."
> ❌ "Lightning-fast turnaround that will blow your mind!"

### Specific
Names the sector, the brand, the deliverable, the timeline, the outcome. Avoids vague claims.

> ✅ "30 assets delivered over 6 months for Hilti — across product launches, internal comms, and channel content."
> ❌ "We've delivered countless videos for global clients across many industries."

### Helpful
Tells the reader what to do next. One clear next step per section. Makes the decision easier, not harder.

> ✅ "Talk to Szymon about a Tech project. Calendly opens in a new tab."
> ❌ "Reach out and let's see if there's a fit."

### Enterprise-Ready
Professional at every touchpoint. Comfortable with brand review, legal review, MLR, compliance vocabulary. Doesn't simplify for the sake of accessibility — speaks the buyer's language.

> ✅ "MLR-aware production. Brand-safe. Built for omnichannel HCP deployment."
> ❌ "We make medical videos easy and fun."

## 7. Studio Access — first-class offering

Studio Access is the productised ongoing offer. It is **one of the two key "at scale" signals on the site** — the other being the blue-chip brand wall.

### Rules

- Studio Access is a **first-class offering with its own page and navigation presence**, not a sub-product of the studio.
- The page name is **"Studio Access"** — capitalized, with its own URL `/studio-access`.
- **No pricing on the page.** The CTA is "Talk to us about Studio Access".
- **Tagline:** *"Studio Access — ongoing video, motion design and animation on a monthly cadence."*

### What's included

- Editing of existing footage (webinars, demos, founder clips, customer interviews)
- Motion design and animation
- Animated explainers
- Product content
- Cinematic AI image and video generation
- Scoped to volume

### How it works (three-step)

1. **Brief** — Monthly brief aligned to content calendar and channel mix.
2. **Produce** — Dedicated team, AI-native pipeline, human craft on every deliverable.
3. **Deliver** — Predictable output, asset libraries built for reuse across channels.

## 8. Testimonials — strategy

### Sources

- **Google reviews** — keep as a trust signal (rating + count near hero).
- **Clutch reviews** — keep as a trust signal (rating + count near hero).
- **Written testimonials** — from letters, emails, LinkedIn DMs where Szymon has permission to publish. **These are the heavy artillery for direct client conversion.**

### Why written testimonials matter more than star ratings

Star ratings work for low-consideration purchases. For a B2B buyer about to commit thousands of dollars and weeks of project time, a named quote from a peer — a marketing director at Roche, a brand manager at Hilti, a CMO at a fintech — carries more weight than any number of anonymous stars.

### Placement

- **Homepage:** 3–4 strongest testimonials, sector-balanced (one tech, one heavy industry, one pharma if possible).
- **Each sector subpage:** 1–2 sector-relevant testimonials — this is where they earn their keep.
- **Studio Access page:** 1–2 testimonials about ongoing / long-term partnerships.
- **Case studies:** one quote per case study where available.
- **Trust strip near hero:** Google + Clutch ratings only — small, supporting role.

## 9. AI disclosure — approach

This expands `DESIGN.master.md` section 5.6.

- AI is named on the homepage "How we work" section as part of the production methodology — owned, not hidden.
- Individual case studies disclose AI use briefly when relevant.
- **No "AI" badge, no separate AI category, no "AI-generated" branding.**
- Treat AI as **how we work, not what we sell.**
- Detailed AI disclosure clauses live in client contracts, not on the marketing site.

**Core framing (approved):**
> "AI powers. People perfect."
> "AI is the engine. Humans are the drivers."
> "Talk about speed, intelligence, and scale — then show the craft."

## 10. Anti-patterns (do not put on this site)

Drawn from `outline.md` section 12 and 13.

### Visual anti-patterns
- Generic AI-studio visual tropes (generative gradients, glassmorphism, "futuristic" sci-fi type, particle backgrounds, glowing accents).
- Stock-feeling "team" or "office" photography.
- Character-driven 2D as the hero look.
- SaaS-coded visual treatment (purple gradients, icon sets that look like Stripe or Linear knockoffs).

### Business / commercial anti-patterns
- No prices, anywhere on the public site.
- No "AI" hero treatment or AI-branded sections.
- No "we're cheaper because we're in Poland" framing on direct-client pages.
- No headcount claims, no office photos, no "team of N" numbers.
- No timezone-as-USP language on direct-client pages (fine to mention on `/portfolio` as a factual note for agencies).
- No money-back guarantees, no free sample lead magnets, no video cost calculators — these are volume-tier signals that erode the premium-craft position.

### Reference studios

**Visual ceiling (look-and-feel target):**
- Buck — buck.co
- Tendril — tendril.ca
- ManvsMachine — manvsmachine.co.uk

**Craft-at-scale analogues (operator credibility without losing finish):**
- Imaginary Forces — imaginaryforces.com
- Antibody — antibody.tv

**Structural reference only (NOT visual — reject all visual treatment):**
- Motion The Agency — motiontheagency.com — steal the IA only. Reject the SaaS-coded purple gradients, glassmorphism, "from £X" pricing.

**Explicitly avoid:**
- Golden Wolf — too irreverent / youthful for pharma + cybersecurity + heavy industry buyers.
- Ordinary Folk — too boutique-feeling, doesn't signal scale.
